Research

Neurophysiology, motor control, and Para sport classification integrity

Overview

My research programme investigates how neurophysiological measurements — particularly surface electromyography (sEMG) — can be used to objectively characterise impairment in Paralympic athletes and to detect intentional misrepresentation during functional classification assessments.

Classification determines which sport class an athlete competes in, and therefore has significant implications for fairness. Current systems rely heavily on clinical observation, which can be manipulated. I am building signal-processing and machine-learning pipelines that make it harder to "game" the system while remaining practical for real-world use.

Research Themes

Three interconnected pillars of my doctoral work

⚡

1 · Surface Electromyography (sEMG)

sEMG records the electrical activity produced by skeletal muscles during contraction. I use both conventional and high-density electrode grids to capture motor unit action potential trains and global muscle activation envelopes.

Key questions I address with sEMG:

  • How do motor unit recruitment thresholds differ between athletes with and without impairments?
  • Can submaximal effort be reliably distinguished from maximal effort using sEMG features?
  • What is the test-retest reliability of sEMG measures across classification assessment sessions?
High-density EMG Motor unit decomposition Signal reliability Feature extraction
🧠

2 · Motor Control & Neuromuscular Adaptation

Understanding how injury, disease, or limb deficiency alters the way the nervous system organises voluntary movement. I combine EMG with biomechanical measures and clinical assessments to characterise impairment across a spectrum of severity.

Research activities include:

  • Measuring maximal voluntary contraction and force–EMG relationships in Para athletes
  • Characterising inter-limb asymmetry using multi-site EMG
  • Assessing neural drive and common synaptic input to motoneuron pools
MVC Neural drive Inter-limb asymmetry Proprioception
🏅

3 · Para Sport Classification Systems

Paralympic classification assigns athletes to sport classes based on the degree to which an eligible impairment affects sport-specific activities. Despite decades of development, the field lacks robust, objective tools to detect intentional misrepresentation — athletes feigning greater impairment than they possess.

My classification research addresses:

  • Systematic review of existing evidence on measurement of intentional misrepresentation
  • Development of sEMG-based test batteries for functional classification activities
  • Training and validating ML classifiers to distinguish genuine from simulated impairment
  • Proposing policy frameworks for integrating objective tools into classification protocols

Methods & Tools

The experimental and computational approaches I employ

📡

Electrophysiology

Delsys Trigno wireless EMG, OT Bioelettronica HD-EMG grids, force transducers, dynamometry.

🐍

Data Analysis

Python (NumPy, SciPy, scikit-learn, MNE), MATLAB, R for statistical modelling and signal processing.

🤖

Machine Learning

Support vector machines, random forests, gradient boosting, and neural networks for EMG classification.

📐

Biomechanics

3D motion capture, force plates, gait analysis — integrated with EMG for multi-modal assessment.

📚

Systematic Review

PRISMA-compliant reviews of classification measurement literature and psychometric properties of tests.

🧪

Clinical Assessment

Standardised manual muscle testing, functional reach, grip dynamometry per IPC classification rules.
